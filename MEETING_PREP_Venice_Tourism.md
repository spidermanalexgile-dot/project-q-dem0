# Venice Tourism — Meeting Prep (Q&A)

> Quick-reference for likely questions. Numbers are as of early/mid-2025 knowledge — figures marked ⚠️ may have updated; verify before quoting externally.

---

## 30-Second Situation Brief

Venice is the global poster child for overtourism. Roughly **20–25M+ visitors a year** (some counts push toward 30M including all day-trippers) against a historic-centre resident population that has **collapsed below 50,000** — down from ~175,000 in 1951. About **75–80% of visitors are day-trippers** who strain infrastructure but spend little. In **April 2024** Venice became the **first city in the world to charge a day-tripper access fee** (€5 on peak days, booked via QR/website). Cruise ships over 25,000 tons were **banned from the Giudecca Canal in 2021** (rerouted to Marghera). UNESCO has repeatedly threatened to list Venice as **"World Heritage in Danger."** The €6bn **MOSE flood barriers** (against *acqua alta*) have been operational since 2020.

---

## The Problem

**Q: How bad is it, in numbers?**
~20–25M+ visitors/year; historic-centre population under 50,000 and still falling; on peak days day-trippers vastly outnumber residents. ~75–80% are day-trippers.

**Q: Why are day-trippers specifically the issue?**
They impose the bulk of the strain — waste, cleaning, crowd management, wear on bridges and monuments — but contribute little economically. Many arrive with packed lunches, stay a few hours, and leave. High footprint, low spend.

**Q: What's happening to residents?**
Exodus. Housing converted to short-term tourist rentals (Airbnb), rising cost of living, everyday shops replaced by souvenir stores, and quality-of-life erosion. The historic centre dropped below 50,000 residents in 2022 (vs ~175,000 in 1951).

---

## What Venice Has Already Tried

**Q: What measures are in place?**
1. **Day-tripper access fee** — launched April 2024, €5 on ~29 peak days, booked via website/QR. World-first.
2. **Cruise ship ban** — ships >25,000 tons banned from the Giudecca Canal/lagoon (2021), rerouted to Marghera.
3. **Tour group caps** — max 25 people per group; loudspeaker ban (2024).
4. **Crowd monitoring** — turnstiles and people-counting at key choke points (controversial).
5. Restrictions and debate on new hotels and short-term rentals.

**Q: Did the day-tripper fee actually work?** ⚠️
Contested. The 2024 run raised gross revenue (~€2.4M reported) but administration costs were high, and critics argued it **did not meaningfully reduce crowds** — it behaved more like a revenue tool than a deterrent. 2025 expanded the number of fee days and introduced **tiered pricing (≈€5 if booked ahead, €10 last-minute)**. Debate continues over whether €5–10 is high enough to change behaviour.

**Q: Why the cruise ban, and what changed?**
Large ships caused wave damage, pollution, and dwarfed the skyline. After years of pressure and a UNESCO "in danger" threat, Italy banned the largest ships from the Giudecca Canal in 2021, rerouting them to Marghera. Cruise tourism **shifted rather than disappeared** — passengers still arrive and shuttle in.

## The Hard / Skeptical Questions (tie back to Project Q)

**Q: Venice already has a fee. What does Project Q add?**
The current €5 fee is a flat deterrent/revenue tool experienced as **pure cost** — resented and barely behaviour-changing. Project Q is different on two axes: (1) it's **dynamic** — the fee scales to actual impact via the DPM, not a flat token; (2) it **returns the fee to the visitor as spendable QCash** at local businesses. That converts a resented tax into a positive experience *and* channels spending into the local economy — directly attacking the "day-trippers don't spend" problem.

**Q: If you give the fee back as QCash, how is it sustainable / how does the city make money?** *(the key challenge)*
The fee recirculates as local spending power, which (a) drives revenue to local businesses — fixing the core complaint that day-trippers contribute nothing economically; (b) lets the system capture value through margin, float, and data; (c) lets the dynamic model price at the level that actually **shifts demand across time and space** rather than a flat amount. Honest version: the unit economics depend on the DPM plus the local-business network — which is exactly what the demo proves out.

**Q: Why would a day-tripper download an app for a few hours?**
Because the fee is mandatory anyway and the app is how they **reclaim its value**. Near-zero friction — no signup, tied to the arrival QR. "Spend the €40 you already paid before you leave tonight" is a strong hook for a cruise or train day-tripper. (This is precisely what Day Tripper mode demonstrates.)

**Q: Isn't this just a tax dressed up?**
The difference is the value loop. A tax extracts; this **recirculates** money into the local economy and gives the visitor agency and benefit. It aligns incentives: visitor gets value, businesses get spend, city manages flows.

**Q: Why Venice and not Queenstown?**
Trevor's interest, and Venice is the world's most recognised overtourism case — instant credibility and a real, active policy environment (the fee already exists, so we're improving a live system, not inventing one). The DPM confidence band for Venice is a key thing we're still validating.

## Landmines — Be Careful

- **Don't overstate the fee's success.** It's contested; it raised revenue more than it cut crowds. Frame Project Q as solving what the flat fee didn't.
- **Don't claim the fee reduced crowds** — the evidence is weak.
- **Verify the 2025/2026 fee figures and revenue** before quoting externally — they may have moved since my knowledge.
- **Population:** use "<50,000 historic centre." Don't confuse with Greater Venice (~250k including mainland Mestre).
- If pressed on exact current numbers you're unsure of, say "the latest published figure I have is X — I'll confirm the current number" rather than guessing.
